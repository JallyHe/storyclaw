This skill converts scene planning documents into full screenplay format with dialogue and action lines, then optimizes the dialogue using dramatic techniques.

## Core Principle: Two-Phase Process

Phase 1 and Phase 2 are strictly separated. Phase 1 must be complete before Phase 2 begins. Phase 1 gets the story RIGHT — correct logic, complete information, proper order. Phase 2 makes the dialogue GOOD — injecting dramatic techniques onto the correct foundation.

---

## Phase 1: Complete the Screenplay (Logic First)

Goal: Output a logically complete, informationally correct, properly ordered screenplay draft. Do NOT pursue clever dialogue, dramatic action, or subtext depth. Only pursue: everything that needs to be said IS said, everything that needs to happen HAS happened, the order is correct.

### Step 1: Read the Scene Plan
Understand each scene's: Content Summary (what happens), Function Analysis (why it's here), Dramatic Points (where conflict lives), Information Flow (who learns what), and the global Information Release Rhythm.

### Step 2: Write Each Scene in Order
- Each scene strictly fulfills the functions marked in the scene plan
- Every item in the Information Flow is represented in dialogue or action
- Dialogue is FUNCTIONAL: characters speak to convey information, express positions, advance plot. Do not pursue rhetoric or subtext
- Action is INDICATIVE: characters act because the plot requires it. Do not pursue dramatic tension in actions
- Characters maintain basic consistency (personality, status, emotion) but do not pursue refined voice differentiation

### Step 3: Logic Self-Check
After all scenes drafted, verify:
- Is information complete? Is every required piece of information from the scene plan present?
- Is the storytelling order correct? Are suspense elements built and revealed at the right scenes?
- Are character behaviors consistent with their core traits?
- Is scene-to-scene causality smooth?

**Phase 1 completion mark: A logically complete first draft — information nothing missing, order nothing wrong, causality nothing broken.**

---

## Phase 2: Optimize Dialogue & Action (Technique Injection)

Goal: On top of the logically correct draft, inject dramatic techniques scene by scene. The optimization unit is ONE SCENE — complete one before moving to the next.

Techniques are applied in five layers — from macro structure to micro polish. Each layer builds on the previous. Do NOT skip layers or apply out of order.

---

### Layer 1: Character Foundation

#### Technique 1: Voice Differentiation (人物声音差异化)
Establish who is speaking before anything else. If names are hidden, can the speaker be identified from dialogue alone? Each character should have unique vocabulary, sentence length, speech rhythm, and speech habits. Without this foundation, all subsequent techniques become "the author speaking through characters."

---

### Layer 2: Core Principles — The Three Pillars

#### Technique 2: Subtext First (潜台词优先)
Characters rarely say what they really mean directly. Use hints, avoidance, topic shifts, irony, and actions to encode meaning. The audience should feel the gap between what is said and what is meant.

**Iron law: Subtext changes HOW information is delivered, not WHETHER it is delivered.** Encoding is not cropping. After optimization, verify every piece of original information (plot, motivation, relationship, foreshadowing) is still receivable by the audience.

#### Technique 3: Meta-Commentary (元评论)
Characters should not just "speak" — they continually "comment on what they're saying and what's happening." Meta-commentary is subtext's most common vehicle: it provides a safe shell, hiding real emotion behind commentary on others or the situation.

Forms: self-mockery, commenting on another's words before answering, labeling the current situation, surface praise with hidden edge, meaningful silence.

#### Technique 4: Conflict Is Drama (冲突即戏剧)
Every exchange must contain opposition. If two characters agree throughout a scene, either the scene is broken or the agreement conceals deeper conflict. Conflict is the engine that drives dialogue — without it, dialogue becomes information broadcasting.

Forms: positional conflict (want different things), informational conflict (know different things), status conflict (competing for control), internal conflict (character's own contradiction leaking into speech).

---

### Layer 3: Information Strategy

#### Technique 5: Information Release (信息层层递进)
Information must not be dumped all at once. Peel the onion layer by layer. Each line should reveal only what the audience needs at that exact moment — no more, no less. Control the gap between what the audience knows and what each character knows.

#### Technique 6: Q&A Role Reversal (问答角色翻转)
Break the one-asks-one-answers pattern. Let the character who should ask answer themselves. Let the character who should answer ask instead. Let each character's personality — not the plot's information needs — determine how information is delivered.

---

### Layer 4: Dramatic Techniques

#### Technique 7: Callback / Echo (回声呼应)
Reference earlier lines or actions in later moments. Three types: quoting another's words to rebut, repeating one's own words for emphasis, having a third party repeat for satire or empathy. Requires material seeded by earlier techniques to recycle.

#### Technique 8: Face-Slap (打脸/言行不一)
Create moments where a character's words are immediately contradicted by reality — what they just claimed is exposed as false by action or circumstance. Requires a consistent behavioral pattern already established (via Technique 1).

#### Technique 9: Tension Break (紧张断点)
At the moment of highest tension, insert an extremely brief interruption completely unrelated to the dialogue — an external intrusion, an object accident, or a mundane action. Limit: no more than twice per scene; no longer than three lines. Requires existing tension (built by Technique 4).

---

### Layer 5: Polish & Finishing

#### Technique 10: Action Catalyzing Dialogue (动作催化对白)
Place action descriptions before key lines (energy buildup), after key lines (emotional release), or within key lines (rhythm pause). Action and dialogue catalyze each other — neither is complete alone.

#### Technique 11: Rhythm Control (节奏控制)
Adjust speech speed and pause placement to match scene emotion. Tighten for urgency, loosen for reflection. A scene's dialogue rhythm is its heartbeat — if every scene speaks at the same pace, the audience stops feeling.

#### Technique 12: Trim Redundancy (删减冗余)
Cut what can be cut — pleasantries, repeated content, filler. If an action can express it, don't use dialogue.

**Iron law: What you delete is redundant EXPRESSION, not information.** Before cutting, verify: what information does this line carry? Does it exist elsewhere? Will the audience still have everything they need to understand what follows? After trimming, run the information loss check before the rhythm check — losing information is an accident; losing rhythm is fixable.

---

**Phase 2 completion mark: A complete screenplay that, on a logically correct foundation, has distinctive character voices, layered subtext, sustained conflict, controlled information flow, precise dramatic beats, and tight rhythm — with zero information loss.**

## Standard Screenplay Format

```
【场景 X：地点 —— 时间（白天/夜晚/黄昏等）】

人物表：人物A、人物B

△ 场景描写（环境、氛围、人物初始状态）

人物A
（动作/情绪提示）
对白内容……

人物B
（动作/情绪提示）
对白内容……

△ 转场提示或关键动作描写
```

- Scene titles use 【】 with location and time
- Action descriptions start with △
- Character names are standalone, followed by dialogue
- Emotion/action cues go in parentheses before dialogue
- No quotation marks around dialogue
